Financial Aid Planning

Why this step matters

Notice that this step comes before essay writing. That's deliberate. For most families, financial aid decides where you can actually enroll — so we plan the money before we polish a single essay. Knowing each school's real cost also shapes your list from Step 3: some "expensive" private colleges will turn out cheaper than your local public option once aid is applied.

The key idea is net price: the sticker price minus grants and scholarships you don't repay. Every college is required to publish a Net Price Calculator. Run it for every school on your list, using your family's real numbers — you'll need your parents' Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) from their most recent tax return.

Know the main pieces: FAFSA (the federal aid form every student files), CSS Profile (an extra form many private colleges like Stanford require), the California Dream Act Application (state aid for eligible undocumented California students who can't file FAFSA), fee waivers (most families with modest incomes pay $0 in application fees), and QuestBridge (a program that matches high-achieving, low-income students to full four-year scholarships at top colleges — including Stanford and MIT).

Your action items

  1. Sit down with your parents and gather the financial numbers. You need: their AGI from the most recent tax return, and a simple asset summary (cash/CDs, stocks/investments, 529 plans, any property other than the primary home) for both parent and student. Also check: if you worked and received a W-2, ask whether your income was included in the family return — this matters for FAFSA. Put all of it in a private tab of your Google Sheet. Do this in early fall of senior year at the latest; September is ideal.
  2. Run the Net Price Calculator for every college on your list. Find it on each school's financial aid website (or via the college's page on College Board). Save each result as a PDF for future reference and record the net price in your Google Sheet next to that college. Compare: you may find a private school with generous aid beats a public school's price.
  3. Create your accounts and check QuestBridge eligibility — before October 1. Create your FAFSA account at studentaid.gov (note: one of your parents needs their own separate account too — plan a time to do this together). If any of your colleges require the CSS Profile, confirm your College Board account works (you already have one if you've taken AP exams). FAFSA and the California Dream Act Application open October 1 — aim to submit as close to the opening date as possible. And check QuestBridge eligibility now: if your household earns under roughly $65,000 (family of four) and you earn mostly A's in challenging courses, the National College Match could change everything — but its deadline is late September, so it must be prioritized.
Family check-in: This step simply cannot happen without your parents. Schedule a specific evening to collect tax and asset information together, and explain why: every dollar of aid starts with these forms. If a relative (like an uncle) helps with taxes, get clarifications from them early.
Google Sheet reminder: Add a "Financial Aid" tab: college, net price (from calculator), FAFSA required (Y), CSS Profile required (Y/N), fee waiver status, aid deadlines. Save every Net Price Calculator result as a PDF too.

Trusted resources

FAFSA (studentaid.gov)
The starting point for all federal and most state aid — grants, work-study, and loans. Opens October 1 each year. Both you and a parent need accounts, so set them up early.
CSS Profile (College Board)
Required by many private colleges (Stanford, USF, Santa Clara, most Ivies) in addition to FAFSA. Fee waivers are automatic for lower-income families. Check each private college's aid page to confirm whether it's needed.
Net Price Calculator Center
Links to every college's official Net Price Calculator. This is how you learn the real cost. Example worth studying: Stanford's affordability page — very good aid for families earning under $150K/year.
California Dream Act Application
State financial aid (including Cal Grants) for eligible California students who can't file FAFSA due to immigration status. Also opens October 1, with a March 2 priority deadline for Cal Grants.
QuestBridge National College Match
Matches high-achieving students from households earning under ~$65K to full four-year scholarships at 50+ top colleges including Stanford, MIT, and many selective engineering schools. Assess the eligibility criteria honestly — if you match, this application takes priority over everything else in September.
Fee waivers: UC · Cal State · Common App
Based on family income, you may pay nothing to apply. UC waives fees for up to four campuses; Cal State's waiver is built into the application; Common App has a fee-waiver checkbox your counselor confirms. Never let application fees shrink your list before checking these.
Tie it to a date: QuestBridge NCM — late September. FAFSA, CA Dream Act, and CSS Profile — open October 1; submit within the first weeks. Cal Grant priority deadline — March 2. Every one of these is on the Deadline Calendar.
