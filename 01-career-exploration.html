Step 1 of 10

Career Exploration

Why this step matters

Everything in this journey works backward from one question: what kind of work do you want to do with your life? Your career interest points you to the right majors (Step 2). Your major points you to the right colleges (Step 3). And knowing your target early gives us time to plan the money side (Step 4) before any essay is written.

Students who skip this step often pick colleges by name or ranking, then discover the school doesn't even offer a pathway to the career they want. Not every college offers a route to becoming, say, a physician's assistant or an aerospace engineer. Do this research first and you'll save yourself months of backtracking.

You don't need to be 100% certain. You need two or three serious career interests, researched well enough to discuss with me and your parents.

Start your Google Sheet now. Create one personal Google Sheet to track all research and application information in a single place. Add a "Careers" tab with columns for: career name, what the day-to-day work looks like, median pay, job growth outlook, education required, and your honest interest level (1–5). Share the sheet with your coach. You will use this same sheet through every step, all the way to enrollment.

Your action items

  1. Pick 3–5 careers that genuinely interest you and research each one on the BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook. For each career, note the median pay, the job growth outlook, the typical entry-level education, and what people in that job actually do all day. Add every finding to your Google Sheet. Aim to finish this within two weeks so we can discuss it at our next meeting.
  2. Talk to at least one real person in each of your top two careers. A family friend, a teacher's contact, a professional your parents know, or someone at a local hospital, firm, or company. Ask them: What does a typical day look like? What education did you need? What do you wish you had known in high school? Write their answers in your Sheet.
  3. Discuss your top choices with your parents and make sure they are aligned. Your family will be your partners through this whole journey — especially on cost. Share your Sheet with them and walk them through why each career interests you. Bring their questions to our next session.
Family check-in: Career choice affects how long you'll be in school and how much it will cost (a physician's assistant needs graduate school; many engineering careers need only a bachelor's degree). Your parents need to see this picture early, not after applications are submitted.

Trusted resources

BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook
The single best free career research tool. Published by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, it gives you real data on pay, job growth, required education, and daily work for hundreds of careers. Start every career search here. Example: the physician assistant page shows exactly what education and licensing the career requires.
CareerOneStop
A U.S. Department of Labor site with short career videos and self-assessment quizzes — helpful if you're starting from "I have no idea" and need to narrow the field before diving into BLS data.
CollegeVine
Create a free account now. Its career-to-major guides help you see which majors lead to which careers — the exact bridge you'll need for Step 2. You'll also use CollegeVine heavily for college list-building later.
O*NET OnLine
Goes one level deeper than BLS: exact skills, tools, and personality traits each career uses. Great for comparing two similar careers side by side (for example, physician assistant vs. registered nurse).
Tie it to a date: Ideally this step happens in 9th–11th grade, but it's never too late. If you're a rising senior, complete it before August 1 — that's when the Common App and UC application open, and the rest of the timeline moves fast. See the Deadline Calendar.
