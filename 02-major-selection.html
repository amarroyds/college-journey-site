Major Selection

Why this step matters

In Step 1 you identified careers that excite you. Now we work backward one more step: which college majors actually lead to that career?

This matters more than most students realize. Not all colleges offer a pathway to every career — a student aiming to become a physician's assistant needs a strong pre-health major like Biology; a student aiming for aerospace needs a school with a real engineering program, not just "physics." And when you get to applications, the UCs, Cal States, and UT campuses ask you to apply to a specific major — some majors (especially engineering and computer science) are far more competitive than the campus overall. Choosing your major now shapes your entire college list in Step 3.

Your action items

  1. List your top 3 majors related to your career choice. Go back to the BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook page for your career and read the "How to Become One" section — it names the typical majors and degrees. For each major, note in your Google Sheet: the career(s) it leads to, whether graduate school is required after, and any required licensing or exams.
  2. Identify one backup major for each primary major. Competitive majors like Computer Science, Nursing, and most Engineering programs reject strong students every year. Know your alternate path at each school (for example: Bioengineering as primary, Biology as alternate). Add a "Backup major" column to your Sheet.
  3. Walk your parents through your major choices and how they connect to your career plan. Explain the education timeline — a 4-year bachelor's vs. bachelor's plus graduate school — and what each path means for total cost. Make sure they are aligned before you start building your college list.
Google Sheet reminder: Add a "Majors" tab with columns: major, careers it leads to, grad school needed (Y/N), example colleges offering it, backup major. You'll reuse this tab constantly in Step 3.

Trusted resources

BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook
Read the "How to Become One" tab for each of your careers — it tells you exactly which degrees employers expect, which is the most reliable way to pick a major.
CollegeVine
Its major guides explain what you'll study, typical career outcomes, and which colleges are strong in that major. Use the same free account you created in Step 1.
College Board BigFuture — Explore Majors & Careers
College Board's free tool connects majors to careers and to the colleges that offer them. You'll need a College Board account later anyway (for the CSS Profile in Step 4, and you already have one if you've taken AP exams).
Niche — Best Colleges by Major
Ranks colleges within each major and includes real student reviews. Remember: you won't get all information from a single website — cross-check what you find here with the college's own site.
UC Campuses & Majors search
The official list of every major at every UC campus. Essential if the UCs are on your list — the same major can be very different (and differently competitive) from one campus to another.
Tie it to a date: Finish your major list before you build your college list — ideally by early summer before senior year, and no later than mid-August. Applications open August 1 (Common App, UC, ApplyTexas) and October 1 (Cal State Apply). See the Deadline Calendar.
