Step 8 of 10

Scholarship Search & Essays

Why this step matters

Financial aid from FAFSA and the colleges (Step 4) covers the big picture — but private scholarships fill the gaps: books, transportation, a laptop, fees that aid doesn't cover. For a commuting student, a few $500–$2,000 local scholarships can be the difference between working 25 hours a week and having time to actually study.

The good news: you've already done most of the work. Your activities inventory (Step 6) and your polished essays (Step 5) are exactly what scholarship applications ask for. A strong Common App essay can be adapted for many scholarship prompts. Scholarship season runs alongside and after your college applications — start searching while you wait for decisions, roughly November through March.

A rule that protects you: legitimate scholarships never charge an application fee. If a "scholarship" asks for money or a credit card, it's a scam. Close the tab.

Your action items

  1. Build a scholarship pipeline in your Google Sheet. Create a "Scholarships" tab: scholarship name, amount, due date, eligibility, application requirements (essay? letters? transcript?), status, and result. Capture the due dates and requirements the moment you find each one. Prioritize local scholarships first — your school counselor's list, city and county organizations, your parents' employers, community foundations, and local branches of groups like Rotary. Local awards have far fewer applicants than national ones.
  2. Apply on a steady rhythm: 1–2 scholarships per week from November through March. Reuse and adapt your existing essays — most prompts overlap with what you've already written ("describe a challenge," "describe your goals"). Adapt genuinely for each prompt, then polish with Hemingway and Grammarly as always. Small awards add up; don't skip the $500 ones.
  3. Track every deadline and loop in your parents. Some scholarships need a parent's tax info or employer verification, and some local ones require attending an award event. Review your Scholarships tab with your parents monthly so nothing slips.
Google Sheet reminder: The Scholarships tab is a pipeline, not a wish list — every entry needs a due date and a status. Review it at every coaching meeting.

Trusted resources

College Board BigFuture Scholarship Search
A large, reputable database that filters by your background, major, and location. BigFuture also runs its own monthly scholarship drawings just for completing planning steps.
QuestBridge
Beyond the National College Match (Step 4), QuestBridge Regular Decision and its partner-college scholarships remain open to low-income students. If you created a QuestBridge account earlier, check what you still qualify for.
Fastweb
One of the oldest scholarship matching services — creates a profile once, then emails you matching scholarships with deadlines. Use a dedicated email folder so your inbox stays sane.
Scholarships.com
A second large database to cross-check Fastweb — different scholarships surface on different sites, same as college research: you won't get everything from a single website.
Your high school counseling office
The most underrated source. Counselors keep lists of local scholarships that never appear in national databases — and local means better odds. Ask for the list in November and again in January; new ones arrive all year.
Tie it to a date: Start searching in November (right after applications submit), apply steadily through March, and check each college's own merit scholarship deadlines — some require a separate application due as early as December. See the Deadline Calendar.
