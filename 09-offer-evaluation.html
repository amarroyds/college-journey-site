Step 9 of 10

Offer Evaluation & Financial Aid Negotiation

Why this step matters

Decisions arrive between December (Early Action, QuestBridge Match) and late March (UC and Regular Decision). Each acceptance comes with a financial aid offer — and offers are not final numbers. Colleges expect appeals, and a respectful, well-documented request can add thousands of dollars per year. But you can only compare and negotiate well if you read each offer correctly.

Learn to separate the parts of every aid letter: grants and scholarships (free money — this is the number that matters), work-study (money you must earn during the year), and loans (money you repay with interest). Some letters blur these together to look generous. Your true cost at each school is: total cost of attendance, minus grants and scholarships only. That's the number that goes in your Sheet — and the number your family decides with.

Also compare offers against the Net Price Calculator estimates you saved back in Step 4. If an offer comes in well below the calculator's estimate, that alone is grounds to appeal.

Your action items

  1. Build a side-by-side comparison in your Google Sheet. Create an "Offers" tab: college, total cost of attendance, grants/scholarships, work-study, loans, net cost to family (cost minus free money only), commuting savings if you'd live at home, and the four-year picture (aid can change after freshman year — ask each school if the aid is renewable and what GPA it requires). Enter every offer within a week of receiving it.
  2. Appeal where the numbers don't work — politely, in writing, with documents. Two strong grounds: (a) your family's finances changed since the tax year FAFSA used (job loss, medical costs, a new dependent), or (b) a comparable college offered you meaningfully more. Email the financial aid office (this is usually called a "professional judgment review" or appeal), attach documentation, and be specific: "An additional $4,000/year in grant aid would make attendance possible." Never invent numbers, and never treat it as a bargaining war — one respectful, documented request. Your coach can review the email draft before you send it.
  3. Review everything with your parents before responding to any college. Walk through the Offers tab together. Make no commitment to any school — including attractive direct-admission or rolling offers — until your parents agree and you've received all decisions you're waiting on. It's fine to tell a college "we're waiting on other offers"; every family does this in April.
Family check-in: This is the biggest family decision of the journey. Schedule a family meeting in early April, put the Offers tab on the screen, and decide the affordability line together — before emotions about "dream schools" take over.

Trusted resources

studentaid.gov — How aid is calculated
Official explanation of your Student Aid Index (SAI) and how schools build aid packages — the vocabulary you need to read offers and write appeal letters.
Net Price Calculator Center
Re-run each school's calculator and compare against the PDFs you saved in Step 4. A big gap between the estimate and the actual offer is your strongest appeal evidence.
California Student Aid Commission
Check your Cal Grant status through WebGrants for Students — state grants are part of your offer at every UC and Cal State, and errors do happen. Confirm the Cal Grant appears in each California offer.
CollegeVine — aid appeal guides
Free, realistic guides on comparing aid offers and writing appeal letters, with sample language you can adapt (in your own words, as always).
studentaid.gov — Understanding loans
Before accepting any loan in an offer, understand the difference between subsidized, unsubsidized, and PLUS loans. A good rule of thumb: total borrowing across four years shouldn't exceed your expected first-year salary (check it on the BLS Handbook — the same tool from Step 1).
Tie it to a date: Most decisions arrive December–March. Appeals should go out within 2–3 weeks of receiving an offer — and everything must resolve before the May 1 enrollment deadline in Step 10. See the Deadline Calendar.
